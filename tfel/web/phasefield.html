Phase field approach of brittle fracture with the Cast3M finite element solver

T. Helfer

13/01/2017

This article describes how to implement the phase field approach using MFront and the Cast3M finite element solver.

Description of the phase field approach

The physical idea underlying the phase field approach of brittle fracture dates back to the pioneering work of Francfort and Marigo (see Francfort and Marigo (1998)). Bourdin et al.Â were the first to propose a numerical implementation of the approach by regularizing the cracks (see Bourdin, Francfort, and Marigo (2000)).

The description of the phase field approach used here relies on the work of Miehe et al. (see Miehe, Hofacker, and Welschinger (2010))

Defining a volumic energy associated to fracture

The crack network \(\Gamma\) contained in a body \(B\) can be regularized by the following operator defining a damage field \(d\):

\[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} d(x)-l^2 \triangle d(x) &= 0 \ \text{on} \ B \\ d(x) &= 1 \ \text{on} \ \Gamma \\ \nabla d(x).n &= 0 \ \text{on} \ \partial B \end{aligned} \right. \]

where (B) the boundary of \(B\). This operator introduces a characteristic length \(l\).

The crack surface \(\Gamma\) can be computed by the following integral:

\[ \Gamma\left(d\right)=\int_{B}\gamma\left(d\right)\,\mathrm{d}V \]

where \(\gamma (d)=\frac{d^{2}}{2\,l}+\frac{l}{2}\,\vec{\nabla} d\,\cdot\,\vec{\nabla}{d}\)

Let \(g_{c}\) the energy associated with the creation of a unit surface of crack. The amount of energy \(E_{c}\) that was required to create the crack network is:

\[ E_{c}=g_{c}\,\int_{B}\gamma\left(d\right)\,\mathrm{d}V=\int_{B}g_{c}\,\gamma\left(d\right)\,\mathrm{d}V \]

\(g_{c}\,\gamma\left(d\right)\) is an energy per unit of volume associated with the creation of crack network. This quantity is the fundamental brick when building a thermodynamically consistent approach to the phase field modelling of brittle failure.

In pratice, the crack network is an unknown of the mechanical problem, so the idea of the phase field approach is to introduce the damage field \(d\) and add \(g_{c}\,\gamma\left(d\right)\) in the free energy of the material. Application of the Claussis-Duhem inegality then leads to an explicit equation satisfied by the damage field, as described below.

Choice of the free energy

To motivate the choice of the free energy for the phase-field behaviour, we begin to recall some classical results about the decomposition of the free energy of an isotropic elastic material into a positive and negative parts.

Decomposition in positive and negative parts of the free energy of an isotropic elastic material

The free energy \(\Psi^{\mathrm{el}}\) of an isotropic elastic material is given by:

\[ \Psi^{\mathrm{el}}=\lambda\,{\mathrm{tr}\left(\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right)}^{2}+2\,\mu\,\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\,\colon\,\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}} \]

where \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) are the LamÃ© coefficients of the material.

This free energy can be decomposed in a positive part associated with tension and a negative part associated with compression:

\[ \Psi^{\mathrm{el}}=\Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,+}+\Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,-} \]

where \(\Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,+}\) and \(\Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,-}\) are defined by: \[ \left\{ \begin{aligned} \Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,+}&= 2\,\mu\,\left<\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right>_{+}\,\colon\,\left<\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right>_{+}+\lambda\,\left<{\mathrm{tr}\left(\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right)}\right>_{+}^{2}\\ \Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,-}&= 2\,\mu\,\left<\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right>_{-}\,\colon\,\left<\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right>_{-}+\lambda\,\left<{\mathrm{tr}\left(\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right)}\right>_{-}^{2} \end{aligned} \right. \]

In this expression, the positive part of a scalar is defined by: \[ \left<x\right>_{+}= \left\{ \begin{aligned} x & \text{ if } x>0 \\ 0 & \text{ if } x\leq0 \end{aligned} \right. \]

The positive part of a tensor is defined as an isotropic function as follows: \[ \left<\underline{x}\right>_{+}=\sum_{i=1}^{3}\left<\lambda_{i}\right>_{+}\,\underline{n}_{i} \]

Definition of the free energy for the phase-field behaviour

To take into choi The free energy \(\Psi\) is choosen to have the following form:

\[ \Psi=\left(m(d) + k\right)\,\Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,+}{} + \Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,-}{}+g_{c}\,\gamma\left(d\right) \]

where:

  • \(m(d) = \left(1-d\right)^2\) describe the effect of damage on the positive part of the energy.
  • \(k\) is a small constant to ensure a minimal stiffness to the fully damaged material.

Definition of the stress

The stress is defined as the thermodynamic force associated with the elastic strain:

\[ \underline{\sigma} =\displaystyle\frac{\displaystyle \partial \Psi}{\displaystyle \partial \underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}} =\left(m(d) + k\right)\,\left(2\,\mu\,\left<\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right>_{+}+\lambda\,\left<{\mathrm{tr}\left(\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right)}\right>_{+}\,\underline{I}\right)+ 2\,\mu\,\left<\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right>_{-}+\lambda\,\left<{\mathrm{tr}\left(\underline{\epsilon}^{\mathrm{el}}\right)}\right>_{-}\,\underline{I} \]

Thermodynamic force associated with the damage

Inspired by thermodynamic arguments, namely the respect of the Clausis-Duhem inegality, Miehe introduces the following equation:

\[ \frac{g_{c}}{l}\left[d - l^2 \triangle d\right] = 2\,(1 - d)\,H \]

where \[ H(x,t) = \max_{\tau \in [0,t]}\left[\Psi^{\mathrm{el}\,+}\left(\tau\right)\right] \]

This definition of \(H\), which appears as the driving force of the damage evolution, has been introduced to described the irreversibility of the crack propagation, altough the demonstration that this choice leads to an increasing value of the damage is not straightforward.
